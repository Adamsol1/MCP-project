# The Government's Climate Strategy for the Foreign Service 2025–2030

## Strategic Framing
Published during Norway's most serious security situation since World War II, this strategy deliberately maintains climate as a top foreign policy priority, arguing that climate change and security are inextricably linked. Norway's foreign service is directed to integrate climate as a cross-cutting consideration across all policy areas — security, energy, trade, humanitarian assistance, and development cooperation.

## Climate as Security Challenge
Norway frames climate change as a direct security threat: droughts, floods, wildfires, sea level rise and degradation of the marine environment contribute to humanitarian crises, migration, and societal instability. The strategy notes that NATO has identified climate change as a key challenge with security implications. Geopolitical changes — including US withdrawal from the Paris Agreement under President Trump — are acknowledged as making international climate cooperation more challenging.

## Key Priority Areas
**Climate, peace and security**: integrating climate risk into security analysis and conflict prevention. **Climate in polar regions**: Arctic climate dynamics have direct implications for Norwegian sovereignty and security. **Green transition and EU cooperation**: Norway's close alignment with EU climate and industrial policy through the EEA agreement. **Climate in development policy**: Norway is a major climate fund donor and forest protection contributor (International Climate and Forest Initiative). **Climate-energy tension**: Norway explicitly navigates the tension between being an active climate actor and a major petroleum exporter.

## Energy Policy Dimension
Norway positions itself as a stable and predictable petroleum supplier with declining emissions, arguing that Norwegian gas contributes to European energy security while enabling the replacement of coal. The government explicitly declines to prepare a "phase-out strategy" for Norwegian oil and gas, instead focusing on emissions reduction from production.

## Implications for Intelligence Collection
Norway's climate foreign policy creates intelligence equities: Norwegian participation in international climate negotiations shapes EU and Nordic positions; Norway's petroleum export role makes it a target for both environmental advocacy pressure and state-actor coercion; Arctic climate-driven changes in sea ice affect Norwegian military geography and Russian Arctic operations.
