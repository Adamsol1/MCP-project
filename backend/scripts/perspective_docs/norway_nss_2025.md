# Norway's National Security Strategy

## First-Ever National Security Strategy
This is Norway's first comprehensive national security strategy, produced in response to what the government describes as the most serious security situation since World War II. It identifies six fundamental security interests: a free and independent Norway, a robust democracy, a safe society with high trust, an open and adaptable economy, allied solidarity and unity in Europe, and a world governed by international law.

## Security Situation Assessment
The strategy identifies five major shifts: Russia has become more dangerous and unpredictable; China's military build-up and coercive measures are creating uncertainty; transatlantic relations have undergone a "major shift" with new US priorities reducing the certainty of extensive US military presence in Europe; the distinction between war and peace is increasingly blurred; and technological competition is intensifying, with dual technology ecosystems emerging.

## Three Strategic Priorities
**Rapidly strengthen defence capability**: Norway must be prepared for war on its territory. Within NATO, Norway will work to ensure Europe assumes far greater responsibility for its own security as US engagement shifts. **Increase resilience of Norwegian society**: whole-of-society approach to reducing vulnerabilities to hybrid threats, disinformation, and covert hostile activities. **Strengthen economic security**: increase competitiveness, reduce dependencies on countries outside Norway's security cooperation, and deepen economic cooperation with allies.

## Key Threat Actors
Russia is explicitly named as a neighbour that "has become more dangerous." The strategy notes Russia receives military support from DPRK and Iran, with China providing political support and materiel — an unusually direct public attribution in Norwegian security policy. China is described as creating "long-term challenges" while still requiring cooperation on global challenges.

## Transatlantic Relations
The strategy is notable for its frank acknowledgement that "a continued extensive US military presence in Europe is not a given" and that "New US priorities have far-reaching implications." Europe must "shoulder a greater share of the cost." Norway frames its response as deepening Nordic cooperation and EU engagement on security.

## Implications for Intelligence Collection
The NSS establishes the political framework for Norwegian intelligence priorities: Russian military and hybrid threats to Norway and the High North, Chinese intelligence activity and economic coercion, transatlantic solidarity risks, critical infrastructure protection, and technology security. The frank acknowledgement of shifting US commitments reflects Norwegian intelligence assessments of Alliance durability.
