# China's National Defense in the New Era

## International Security Situation
The 2019 white paper frames the world as undergoing "profound changes unseen in a century," with intensifying US-China strategic competition, NATO enlargement, and growing instability in conflict regions. China asserts that the US has "provoked and intensified competition among major countries" by adjusting its national security strategy unilaterally and undermining global strategic stability.

## China's Defensive National Defense Policy
China's stated national defense policy is defensive in nature, rooted in a socialist system and peaceful development path. Core objectives include safeguarding sovereignty, unity, and territorial integrity — explicitly including Taiwan, Tibet, and Xinjiang — while rejecting hegemony and military expansion. China maintains its nuclear strategy of self-defense, pledging no first use and maintaining minimum deterrence. Defense expenditure as a proportion of GDP has remained below 2% for over 30 years, which China presents as evidence of restrained intent.

## Missions and Tasks of the Armed Forces
The PLA's missions include safeguarding national sovereignty and maritime rights, maintaining combat readiness under real combat conditions, securing interests in outer space, cyberspace and the electromagnetic domain, countering terrorism, and protecting Chinese overseas interests. The PLA participates in UN peacekeeping and has deployed naval task forces in the Gulf of Aden since 2008.

## PLA Reform and Modernisation
China is restructuring the PLA around a Joint Operations Command Center, transitioning from a land-power-dominated structure to a posture emphasising maritime, space, and cyber capabilities. The goal is integrated joint operational capability and the ability to win "informatised wars." This reform represents the most significant restructuring of the PLA since 1949.

## Implications for Intelligence Collection
The 2019 defense white paper signals PLA's intent to build power-projection capability while maintaining defensive framing. Collection priorities include PLA joint exercise tempo and interoperability development, undersea capabilities (SSBN patrols, attack submarine deployments), counter-space and cyber offensive development, and the gap between stated defensive posture and observed capability growth in Taiwan Strait–relevant systems.
