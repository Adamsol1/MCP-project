# National Threat Assessment 2026

## Overview
The 2026 National Threat Assessment (NTA) is produced by PST (Norwegian Police Security Service) and forms part of a coordinated annual release alongside NIS's FOKUS (foreign intelligence threats) and NSM's RISIKO (cyber and information security threats). PST Director General Beate Gangås frames Norway as facing "its most serious security situation since World War II." The NTA establishes a shared national threat picture for government, institutions, and the public.

## State Actor Threats
Russia is assessed as the primary state threat to Norway, employing a wide range of methods including intelligence operations, cyber attacks, sabotage planning, influence operations, and the use of proxy actors — including criminal networks — to conduct operations with plausible deniability. The blurring of the distinction between war and peace means hostile activities could increase in scale and become more aggressive across all sectors of society. China is also identified as conducting intelligence operations in Norway.

## Key Threat Methods
State actors are assessed to be: conducting intelligence operations targeting government, defence, and critical infrastructure; employing hybrid tactics including disinformation and influence operations; financing radicalised groups and criminal networks for proxy operations; using migration as a deliberate instrument of pressure; and targeting Norwegian petroleum and energy infrastructure.

## Terrorism Threat Landscape
The 2026 NTA assesses that the terrorism threat is more diffuse and complex than before, compounded by the possibility of state actors orchestrating terrorist acts through proxy actors. The most serious threats come from Islamist and right-wing extremists. Anti-government extremism is also identified as contributing to radicalisation. Radicalisation pathways are primarily digital.

## Threat to Dignitaries
A dedicated section addresses threats to Norwegian and foreign dignitaries, reflecting increased concern about politically motivated violence targeting public figures.

## Implications for Intelligence Collection
PST's publicly stated threat picture directly informs allied collection sharing. Key priorities: Russian intelligence activity in Norway (SVR, GRU, FSB), Chinese intelligence operations targeting Norwegian technology and research institutions, the state-criminal proxy nexus, and radicalisation pipelines in Norwegian online communities.
