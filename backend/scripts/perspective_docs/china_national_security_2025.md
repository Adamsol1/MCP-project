# China's National Security in the New Era

## Overview and Holistic National Security Concept
This is the first comprehensive white paper on national security issued since the PRC's founding in 1949, representing the most authoritative public statement of China's security posture across 20 domains. The Holistic National Security Concept — introduced by Xi Jinping in 2014 and now elevated to foundational doctrine — frames security hierarchically: people's security as the ultimate goal, political security as the fundamental task, economic security as the foundation, and military/technological/cultural security as guarantees. The concept covers: politics, military, territory, economy, finance, culture, society, science and technology, cyberspace, ecology, resources, nuclear, outer space, deep sea, polar regions, biotechnology, food, AI, data, and overseas interests.

## Political Security
Political security — specifically the security of the CPC's position as governing party — is framed as the foundational task. External efforts to promote democracy and human rights are explicitly characterised as hostile "westernisation and differentiation strategies." The document warns of "infiltration, sabotage, subversion and secessionism" in Tibet, Xinjiang, Hong Kong and Macao, and positions the Central National Security Commission — chaired by Xi Jinping — as supreme authority over all national security work.

## Economic Security
Economic security is the material foundation of all other domains. Key priorities include eliminating "technological chokepoints" created by US export controls on semiconductors, protecting the renminbi's international role, ensuring food and supply chain security, and establishing sovereignty over data flows and AI governance. China's self-reliance strategy (zili gengsheng) is framed as a national security imperative, directly responsive to US sanctions on Russia and technology decoupling pressure.

## Military and Territorial Security
China explicitly reserves the right to use force for Taiwan reunification while promoting peaceful cross-strait relations as the preferred path. South China Sea and East China Sea patrols are described as routine sovereignty exercises. The document opposes the Indo-Pacific Strategy and any "Asian version of NATO," framing these as threats to regional stability. Military investment in AI-enabled warfare, space capabilities, and naval expansion is presented as purely defensive.

## Technological and Emerging Domain Security
AI, quantum computing, cyberspace sovereignty, space, deep-sea, biotechnology, and polar access are all framed as security domains requiring state control. China's sustained investment in these areas is presented as a defensive necessity against US "technology surprise."

## Implications for Intelligence Collection
This document operationalises Chinese intelligence priorities: collection focuses on protecting CPC legitimacy from internal and external threats, countering technology denial measures, monitoring diaspora communities deemed to threaten political security, and surveilling states pursuing the Indo-Pacific Strategy. Any entity engaging with Taiwan, Tibet, or Xinjiang issues is framed as a security threat, informing MSS and United Front Work Department targeting.
