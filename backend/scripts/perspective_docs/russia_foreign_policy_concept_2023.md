# The Concept of the Foreign Policy of the Russian Federation (2023)

## Strategic Framework
Approved by Presidential Decree No. 229 on 31 March 2023, this Concept is the most explicit articulation of Russian foreign policy ideology since the Soviet collapse. It frames Russia as a "unique country-civilisation" — a vast Eurasian and Euro-Pacific power with a "historically unique mission" to maintain global balance of power and build a multipolar international system. Russia is positioned as one of the "sovereign centres of global development" performing a civilisational role.

## Anti-Western World Order Framing
The Concept frames the US and its "satellites" as using Russia's Ukraine intervention as a pretext to "aggravate the longstanding anti-Russian policy" and "unleash a new type of hybrid war" aimed at weakening Russia "in every possible way." The West is characterised as clinging to an "imbalanced model" of dominance and deploying "illegal instruments" including sanctions, coup promotion, military conflicts, blackmail, and information manipulation. Russia explicitly frames this as a struggle between "historical centres of power" and a natural emergence of multipolarity.

## Multipolar World Order Goals
Russia's priority foreign policy goals include: eliminating "vestiges of domination" by the US and its allies; restoring the UN's central role; enhancing BRICS, SCO, CIS, EAEU, and CSTO as alternative multilateral frameworks; reducing dependence on dollar-denominated systems; developing alternative payment infrastructure; and supporting "the Global South" in resisting Western coercion.

## Near Abroad and Allies
Russia asserts the right to protect allies and partners "irrespective of whether or not the allies and partners receive international recognition" — language covering breakaway regions. Priority assistance goes to the Republic of Abkhazia, South Ossetia, EAEU members, and CIS states maintaining good-neighbour relations with Russia. The concept explicitly authorises "symmetrical and asymmetrical measures" in response to hostile acts threatening Russian sovereignty.

## Strategic Stability and Nuclear Posture
Russia frames Article 51 of the UN Charter as an "adequate and not to be revised" basis for the use of force in self-defence — interpreted broadly. Strategic deterrence, preventing a global war, and managing nuclear escalation risks are explicit priorities. Russia sees itself as the legitimate guarantor of nuclear non-proliferation while opposing Western arms control frameworks that it views as constraining Russia unilaterally.

## Implications for Intelligence Collection
The 2023 Concept operationalises Russian intelligence priorities: undermining NATO solidarity, cultivating anti-Western political movements in Europe and the Global South, supporting BRICS expansion as an alternative to Western-led institutions, sanctions circumvention through alternative payment systems, and countering any international recognition of Russian-occupied territories. The concept's "Russian world" framing authorises intelligence operations against diaspora communities and Russian-speaking populations in neighbouring states.
