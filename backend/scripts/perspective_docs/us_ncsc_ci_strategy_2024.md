# National Counterintelligence Strategy 2024

## Strategic Direction
The 2024 National Counterintelligence Strategy (NCS) — signed by President Biden and the DNI on August 1, 2024 — provides strategic direction for the US federal government's counterintelligence community. It is aligned with the 2022 National Security Strategy and based on the 2022 National Threat Identification and Prioritization Assessment (NTIPA). The strategy updates counterintelligence priorities to address the proliferation of commercially available surveillance tools, vast new personal data ecosystems, and adversary pre-positioning in critical infrastructure.

## Threat Landscape
Foreign intelligence entities (FIEs) and their proxies are assessed as persistently pursuing: US sensitive information, technology, and intellectual property; surveillance of US persons through commercial data and tools; disruption of US infrastructure, industries, and institutions; and influence operations targeting US policy and public opinion. Non-state actors increasingly employ the same tactics as state FIEs. Hostile intelligence services are assessed as "becoming more capable, aided by increasingly sophisticated and commercially available technical tools."

## Strategic Priorities
The 2024 NCS establishes counterintelligence priorities aligned with the NSS: countering FIE collection against US classified and sensitive programmes; protecting critical infrastructure from adversary pre-positioning; defending against foreign influence operations and malign actors in US civil society; securing US technology and innovation ecosystems; and building a resilient counterintelligence enterprise across government, private sector, and allied nations.

## Key Adversary Focus
China, Russia, Iran, and North Korea are the primary state FIE threats. China's MSS and military intelligence services are assessed as the most active collectors of US technology, personnel data (demonstrated by OPM breach and Volt Typhoon pre-positioning), and economic intelligence. Russia's SVR, GRU, and FSB focus on political influence, military technology, and energy sector intelligence. Iran and DPRK target sanctions evasion, weapons technology, and dissident communities.

## Implications for Intelligence Collection
The NCS shapes US defensive counterintelligence priorities and informs allied CI coordination: joint threat assessments with Five Eyes partners, supply chain integrity programmes, insider threat detection in cleared facilities, and detection of adversary use of commercial surveillance tools against US government personnel.
