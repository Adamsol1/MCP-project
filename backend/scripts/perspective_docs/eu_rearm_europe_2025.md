# ReArm Europe Plan / Readiness 2030

## Overview
The ReArm Europe Plan/Readiness 2030, presented by the European Commission in March 2025, proposes to leverage over €800 billion in defence spending through four main mechanisms: national fiscal flexibility (relaxing EU deficit rules for defence investment), a new €150 billion loan instrument (SAFE — Security Action for Europe) for joint procurement, potential redirection of cohesion funds toward defence, and expanded European Investment Bank support. It also aims to mobilise private capital through the EU Savings and Investments Union.

## SAFE Instrument
The SAFE instrument (€150 billion) provides low-interest loans to member states for joint procurement of defence equipment — with a strong preference for European-sourced capability. Eligible procurement covers air and missile defence, artillery, ammunition, drones, anti-drone systems, and military mobility infrastructure. The instrument is designed to pool demand and overcome the fragmentation of national defence markets.

## Parliamentary Debate and Concerns
The European Parliament broadly supported the ambition but raised concerns about: democratic oversight (use of Article 122 TFEU bypasses ordinary legislative procedure), defence market fragmentation (risk that spending remains nationally siloed), economic sustainability (defence spending crowding out green, social, and R&D budgets), and the need for a long-term strategy rather than emergency measures.

## Alternative Financing Ideas
Debate includes proposals for a dedicated Rearmament Bank or Defence, Security and Resilience Bank that could offer concessional loans and risk guarantees outside the EU budget framework. Grant-based financing and more robust governance structures have also been proposed.

## Implications for Intelligence Collection
The rearmament debate reveals European capability gaps and procurement priorities — which are high-value intelligence targets for state adversaries. Collection focuses on European procurement plans (particularly joint programmes), the pace of member state defence spending increases, SAFE instrument governance and conditionality, and whether European defence industrial capacity can scale to meet demand signals.
