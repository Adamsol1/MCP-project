# Russia–Ukraine War (2022–present)

## Strategic Overview
Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine on 24 February 2022 is the largest conventional military conflict in Europe since World War II. The war has restructured European security, accelerated NATO enlargement, depleted Russian and Western weapons stockpiles, and produced lessons in modern warfare that are reshaping military doctrine globally. As of 2025, the front line has stabilised into attritional warfare broadly following the Donbas axis, with neither side achieving decisive breakthrough.

## Key Phases

### Phase 1: Attempted Decapitation (February–March 2022)
Russia's initial operation aimed for rapid seizure of Kyiv and regime change within days, relying on an intelligence assessment that Ukrainian resistance would collapse. Russian forces advanced on Kyiv from Belarus and the north but were repelled by Ukrainian territorial defence, anti-tank weapons (Javelin, NLAW), and superior local knowledge. Russia lost significant armoured forces and withdrew from northern Ukraine by April 2022.

### Phase 2: Donbas Focus (April 2022–present)
Russia reoriented to a grinding advance in eastern and southern Ukraine (Luhansk, Donetsk, Zaporizhzhia, Kherson oblasts). Both sides settled into attrition warfare characterised by high artillery consumption, drone dominance, and fortified defensive lines. Ukraine conducted successful counteroffensives liberating Kherson (November 2022) and Kharkiv oblast (September 2022).

### Phase 3: Ukrainian Deep Strike (2024–present)
Ukraine developed and deployed long-range strike capabilities (drone attacks on Russian refineries, airfields, and Moscow suburbs) and conducted a limited incursion into Kursk oblast (August 2024) — the first seizure of Russian territory by a foreign force since World War II.

## Military Lessons
- **Drone warfare**: Both tactical reconnaissance drones (FPV suicide drones) and strategic attack drones (Shahed) have become decisive; air defence is being overwhelmed by volume
- **Electronic warfare**: Both sides deploy extensive jamming and spoofing, requiring constant adaptation of drone guidance methods
- **Artillery consumption**: High-intensity conflict consumes ammunition at rates Western arsenals were not designed to sustain
- **Air defence saturation**: Russian missile and drone campaigns against Ukrainian cities demonstrated that layered air defence can be overwhelmed by combined salvo attacks mixing ballistic missiles, cruise missiles, and drones

## Western Support and Escalation Dynamics
Western support evolved from anti-tank missiles and artillery to tanks (Leopard 2, Abrams), long-range rockets (HIMARS, Storm Shadow), and eventually F-16 aircraft. Russia threatened nuclear escalation at each step but did not act. Russia has conducted sabotage operations in EU member states (arson, cable cuts) as sub-threshold retaliation.

## Intelligence Implications
The war has provided real-world validation data on weapons systems, electronic warfare capabilities, and defensive doctrine. Russia's performance revealed pre-war overestimation of Russian conventional military capability. China is assessed to be carefully studying Ukrainian lessons for Taiwan contingency planning. The war established that large-scale conventional warfare in Europe is not only possible but ongoing — fundamentally changing NATO planning assumptions.
