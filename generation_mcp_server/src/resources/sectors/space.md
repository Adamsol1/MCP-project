# Space: Militarisation and Emerging Threats

## Strategic Overview
Space has transitioned from a domain of scientific exploration and communication to a contested military environment where adversaries actively develop and deploy counter-space capabilities. All major powers now field or are developing Anti-Satellite (ASAT) weapons, electronic warfare systems targeting space assets, and surveillance constellations. Satellites underpin modern military operations — GPS navigation, communications, intelligence collection, early warning — making them high-priority targets in any major conflict.

## Anti-Satellite (ASAT) Capabilities

### Russia
Russia demonstrated a direct-ascent ASAT weapon (Nudol system, test November 2021), destroying a defunct Soviet satellite and generating approximately 1,500 trackable debris pieces — a deliberate act that endangered the International Space Station and is widely condemned. Russia also deploys co-orbital systems — satellites that manoeuvre close to adversary satellites for inspection, jamming, or physical attack. Kosmos 2576, launched in 2024, was assessed by the US Space Command as a potential co-orbital weapon deployed near a US classified satellite.

### China
China conducted a direct-ascent ASAT test in 2007, destroying its Fengyun-1C weather satellite and creating the largest debris field in history (~2,800 trackable fragments). China has since developed sophisticated co-orbital capabilities, directed energy weapons (lasers for dazzling or blinding optical sensors), and cyber capabilities targeting satellite ground stations. China's BDS satellite navigation system is a GPS alternative providing positioning data independence.

## Electronic Warfare in Space
GPS jamming and spoofing are mature capabilities deployed by Russia (Baltic region, Syria, Arctic), Iran (GPS spoofing of ships in Persian Gulf), and North Korea. GPS spoofing creates false position data in receivers while appearing as legitimate signal — particularly dangerous for precision navigation and commercial aviation. Airspace and maritime domain awareness has been disrupted repeatedly.

## Starlink as Dual-Use Infrastructure
SpaceX's Starlink constellation (~6,000+ active satellites) provided Ukraine with critical military communications after Russian jamming disrupted traditional networks. Russia has targeted Starlink terminals and attempted to jam signals. Starlink's dual commercial/military role makes it a legitimate military target under the laws of armed conflict, while its commercial satellites complicate that designation. China has proposed "mothership" satellites capable of grabbing adversary satellites.

## Space Debris as Strategic Weapon
ASAT tests create debris that persists for decades, degrading orbital environments for all actors. The Kessler Syndrome — a cascade of collisions generating progressively more debris — is a theoretical threshold that major ASAT attacks could trigger. This creates a "use it or lose it" incentive in crisis: attacking an adversary's satellites before they attack yours, knowing debris may eventually destroy your own assets anyway.

## Intelligence Implications
Adversary space capabilities enable persistent ISR coverage of military movements, early warning circumvention, and precision strike guidance. Counter-space operations against Western GPS, SATCOM, and early warning satellites in a major conflict would severely degrade military effectiveness. Western militaries have begun developing alternative positioning systems, encrypted communications, and space situational awareness to manage this vulnerability.
