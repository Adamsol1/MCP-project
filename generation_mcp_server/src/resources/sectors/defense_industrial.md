# Defence Industrial Base: Threats and Vulnerabilities

## Strategic Overview
The defence industrial base (DIB) of Western nations faces an acute combination of demand surge (driven by the Ukraine war and Indo-Pacific tensions), structural capacity constraints (decades of post-Cold War contraction), and persistent adversary targeting through espionage, supply chain attacks, and talent recruitment. The DIB represents both a strategic enabler and a vulnerability: production rates for critical munitions (155mm artillery shells, Stinger/Patriot missiles) fell below consumption rates in the first year of high-intensity conflict in Ukraine.

## Production Capacity Crisis
Western defence industries contracted significantly after the Cold War, optimising for cost over surge capacity. The Ukraine war exposed critical gaps:
- **Artillery ammunition**: NATO stockpiles depleted within months; European 155mm shell production ramped but still insufficient
- **Air defence missiles**: Patriot, IRIS-T, NASAMS have years-long production queues
- **Shoulder-fired missiles**: MANPADS (Stinger) and ATGM (Javelin) production lines had to be restarted from low-rate production
- **Explosive precursors**: TNT and nitrocellulose supply chains rely heavily on single-source suppliers, including in countries with export restriction risk

## Dual-Use Technology Export Control
Western export control regimes (US ITAR/EAR, EU dual-use regulations) regulate transfer of defence and dual-use technology. Adversaries systematically circumvent these through:
- Shell company networks in third countries (Singapore, UAE, Turkey, Armenia) re-exporting controlled goods
- Academic transfer of theoretical knowledge not covered by export control
- Cyber theft removing the need for physical transfer
- Recruitment of engineers with access to controlled technology

## Foreign Investment in the Defence Sector
China and Russia have attempted to acquire minority stakes in Western defence suppliers to gain technology access or intelligence. Western governments have expanded investment screening (CFIUS in the US, TiSAX in Germany, NSIA in the UK) to block or unwind foreign investment in sensitive DIB companies. The threshold for what constitutes a "defence-related" company has expanded to include semiconductor firms, synthetic biology, and quantum computing.

## Supply Chain Infiltration
The defence supply chain is long, complex, and often opaque. Key risks:
- **Counterfeit components**: Fake semiconductors and electronic components entering the supply chain via grey-market brokers, particularly from China
- **Software supply chain**: Third-party software libraries in defence systems creating dependency on non-vetted code
- **Single-source dependencies**: Many specialty materials (rare earth elements for guidance systems, industrial diamonds for manufacturing) sourced from China

## Intelligence Implications
State-sponsored actors — primarily China, Russia, and Iran — target the DIB for technology collection. Chinese espionage against the US and European DIB has been the largest peacetime technology transfer in history by volume. The emergence of drone warfare has created a new layer of dual-use concern: commercial drone components are directly usable in military systems, and Chinese drone manufacturers (DJI) dominate the global commercial market.
