# Maritime Security: Undersea Infrastructure and Gray-Zone Threats

## Strategic Overview
The maritime domain is an increasingly contested environment beyond traditional naval power projection. Undersea infrastructure (cables, pipelines), commercial shipping routes, and the rules-based maritime order are under pressure from deliberate gray-zone operations, sabotage, and systematic exploitation of AIS (Automatic Identification System) vulnerabilities. The Nord Stream pipeline sabotage (2022) demonstrated that high-value undersea infrastructure is a viable target; subsequent cable cuts across the Baltic Sea and Red Sea have expanded the threat picture.

## Undersea Cable Vulnerability
Approximately 97% of global internet traffic and 10 trillion dollars daily in financial transactions cross undersea cables. There are approximately 400 active undersea cable systems globally. Key vulnerabilities:
- **Physical cutting**: Cables run in known, charted routes and can be cut by a vessel dragging an anchor at relatively low technical sophistication
- **Chokepoints**: Key landing stations and geographic bottlenecks (Strait of Malacca, Red Sea, Baltic) concentrate cable vulnerability
- **Repair time**: Repair ships are scarce (fewer than 60 worldwide), and repairs in contested waters may be impossible during hostilities

### Baltic Sea Cable Incidents
Since 2023, multiple undersea cables in the Baltic Sea have been severed in incidents attributed by Western intelligence to deliberate Russian-linked action — primarily Chinese-flagged vessels dragging anchors over cable routes. Affected cables include Estonia-Finland (2023), Latvia-Sweden, and Germany-Finland connections. These incidents are below the threshold of a casus belli but systematically degrade European resilience.

## AIS Spoofing and Maritime Deception

### Russian Shadow Fleet
Russia operates a "shadow fleet" of 600+ vessels transporting sanctioned Russian oil, predominantly flagged in non-Western registries (Palau, Cameroon, Gabon). Many vessels disable or manipulate AIS transponders to obscure their positions and port calls. This complicates sanctions enforcement and provides cover for intelligence collection vessels operating near sensitive infrastructure.

### GPS/AIS Spoofing
GPS spoofing in the Black Sea, Persian Gulf, and Eastern Mediterranean has caused ships to receive false position data. In the Eastern Mediterranean (particularly near Israel, Cyprus, and Egypt), systematic GPS spoofing has been attributed to Israeli and Russian military systems, creating navigational hazards for commercial shipping. AIS receivers relying on GPS are also affected, creating ghost ship trails in maritime domain awareness systems.

## Houthi Red Sea Campaign
Since November 2023, Houthi forces in Yemen have conducted systematic drone and missile attacks on commercial shipping in the Red Sea using Iranian-supplied weapons. This caused the majority of global container shipping to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10-14 days and significant cost to Asia-Europe routes. The campaign demonstrated that a well-armed non-state actor can impose substantial costs on global maritime commerce with anti-ship missiles and suicide drones.

## Intelligence Implications
Maritime gray-zone operations — cable cutting, AIS manipulation, shadow fleet operations, and port espionage — are systematically employed by Russia, China, and Iran to project power below the threshold of armed conflict. Port access by Chinese-operated terminals globally (COSCO, Hutchison) provides potential collection and logistics infrastructure for PLAN support. Any escalation in the Taiwan Strait or European security environment would immediately translate to maritime domain operations at scale.
