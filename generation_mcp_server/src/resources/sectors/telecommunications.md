# Telecommunications Sector Threat Landscape

## Strategic Overview
Telecommunications infrastructure is both a primary intelligence collection target and a critical vulnerability. Modern military, economic, and social function depends entirely on reliable communications. State actors target telecom infrastructure for signals intelligence collection, pre-positioned disruption capability, and — during hostilities — as a first-strike target.

## Key Threat Vectors

### Undersea Cable Vulnerabilities
Approximately 95% of international internet traffic transits undersea cables. There are roughly 400 active cables globally, concentrated at a small number of chokepoints (Suez Canal approaches, Strait of Malacca, UK–US transatlantic routes). Cable cuts — whether by accident, state sabotage, or covert tapping — have strategic consequences. Russian naval vessels and submarines have been observed surveying cable routes in the Baltic, North Sea, and North Atlantic.

### 5G Supply Chain Security
The integration of Chinese-manufactured equipment (Huawei, ZTE) into 5G infrastructure creates intelligence collection risk: manufacturer access for maintenance can provide persistent presence. Most Western nations have now restricted or banned high-risk vendors from core networks, but legacy 4G equipment in non-core positions remains. The transition creates a prolonged exposure window.

### Signals Intelligence Collection
Telecom infrastructure — particularly international exchange points, submarine cable landing stations, and satellite ground stations — is primary SIGINT collection infrastructure. The Tempora programme (GCHQ) and PRISM (NSA) demonstrated Western capabilities; Russian SORM legislation mandates ISP cooperation with FSB collection; Chinese law requires telecom operators to facilitate state access.

### GPS Jamming and Spoofing
Russia systematically jams and spoofs GPS signals in conflict zones and around sensitive facilities. Norwegian civilian aviation near the Russian border has experienced GPS interference during Russian military exercises. Spoofing — feeding false location data — is more sophisticated and harder to detect than jamming.

## Known Incidents
- Salt Typhoon (China) compromise of US telecom providers including AT&T and Verizon, intercepting law enforcement wiretap systems (2024)
- Undersea cable cuts in the Baltic Sea (BCS East-West Interlink, C-Lion1) following suspected Russian naval activity (November 2024)
- GPS jamming affecting civil aviation across Finland, Estonia, and the Baltic states (2022–present)
- Vodafone Greece illegal wiretapping scandal — Ericsson software exploited for surveillance of Greek officials (2004–2005)

## Intelligence Implications
Telecom infrastructure provides both a collection opportunity and a strategic vulnerability. State actors pre-position for disruption; intelligence agencies exploit access points for collection. The convergence of 5G with critical systems (industrial IoT, emergency services, military communications) significantly raises the stakes of compromise.
