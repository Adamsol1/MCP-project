# North Korea: Financial Operations and Sanctions Evasion

## Strategic Overview
North Korea's nuclear and missile programme requires hard currency that sanctions regimes systematically deny. The DPRK has developed an exceptionally sophisticated state apparatus for generating, laundering, and deploying funds through cryptocurrency theft, financial system exploitation, and complex international trade deception. North Korea's cyber financial operations are uniquely state-directed — the proceeds go directly to fund weapons of mass destruction programmes, making disruption a non-proliferation priority.

## Lazarus Group: Cryptocurrency Theft

### Scale and Methodology
Lazarus Group (also tracked as APT38 for financial operations and BlueNoroff for crypto focus) is responsible for an estimated $3 billion in cryptocurrency theft between 2017 and 2023, including:
- **Ronin Network hack** (2022): $625 million stolen from Axie Infinity's blockchain bridge — the largest crypto hack in history. Attackers gained control of validator private keys through social engineering
- **Harmony Horizon Bridge** (2022): $100 million stolen via compromised multisig keys
- **Atomic Wallet** (2023): $100 million from individual wallets
- **Bybit Exchange** (2025): ~$1.5 billion stolen via supply chain compromise of Safe{Wallet} infrastructure — the largest single crypto theft ever recorded

### Laundering Methodology
DPRK crypto proceeds are laundered through:
1. Conversion to privacy coins (Monero) via DEX (decentralised exchanges) that lack KYC
2. Chain-hopping across multiple blockchains to break traceability
3. Use of "mixers" (cryptocurrency tumblers) such as Tornado Cash (sanctioned by OFAC) to blend funds
4. OTC brokers in China and Southeast Asia converting to fiat or gold
5. Shell company networks purchasing goods for DPRK via third countries

## SWIFT and Banking System Exploitation

### Bangladesh Bank Heist (2016)
Lazarus operators gained access to the Bangladesh Central Bank's SWIFT credentials and submitted fraudulent transfer instructions for $951 million from the bank's Federal Reserve account. $81 million was successfully transferred to accounts in the Philippines before the remainder was blocked. The heist demonstrated that SWIFT's messaging system is only as secure as the endpoints.

### Global ATM Cash-Out Operations ("FASTCash")
DPRK operators have compromised the interbank switch systems of smaller banks in Africa and Southeast Asia, simultaneously manipulating transaction records to authorise fraudulent ATM withdrawals across multiple countries simultaneously. Operations netted tens of millions per incident.

## Trade-Based Money Laundering and Sanctions Evasion
North Korea uses elaborate multi-stage trade schemes:
- Over-invoicing and under-invoicing of goods through third-country intermediaries
- Ship-to-ship oil transfers at sea to obscure the origin of sanctioned petroleum
- Coal and iron ore exports through Chinese intermediaries using falsified certificates of origin
- Use of front companies in Singapore, Malaysia, and China managed by North Korean nationals with foreign residency

## Intelligence Implications
North Korea's financial operations directly fund nuclear warhead and ICBM development — tracing and disrupting these flows is a concrete non-proliferation measure. The Bybit hack demonstrated that even sophisticated institutional crypto platforms are vulnerable to supply chain compromise. North Korea's financial operators have infiltrated Western technology companies through fraudulent remote work arrangements, generating income and stealing intellectual property simultaneously.
