# EU–China Relations

## Strategic Overview
The EU officially characterises China as simultaneously a partner, economic competitor, and systemic rival. The 2021 freeze of the Comprehensive Agreement on Investment (CAI) following mutual sanctions marked a deterioration from which relations have not recovered. China's position on Russia's invasion of Ukraine has deepened EU strategic concern.

## Key Tension Points

### Belt and Road Investment
Chinese BRI investment in EU member states — particularly Greece (Piraeus port), Hungary, and Southern/Eastern European infrastructure — has created asymmetric dependencies. The EU's Global Gateway initiative is its strategic counter. Investment screening mechanisms (EU FDI Regulation, 2020) have tightened but enforcement varies by member state.

### 5G Supply Chain
The EU Toolbox on 5G security recommends excluding high-risk vendors (effectively Huawei and ZTE) from core networks. Implementation is uneven — some member states have moved quickly, others retain significant Chinese equipment in existing deployments. The transition timeline creates a window of exposure.

### Critical Minerals Dependency
China controls approximately 60% of global rare earth mining and 85% of processing capacity. EU dependency on Chinese-refined lithium, cobalt, and rare earths for battery and defence manufacturing is assessed as a strategic vulnerability. The EU Critical Raw Materials Act (2024) aims to reduce this but results are long-term.

### Technology Transfer
Chinese state-linked entities conduct systematic IP theft targeting EU aerospace, defence, pharmaceutical, and semiconductor companies. Academic and research partnerships have been exploited as collection vectors, prompting updated screening of university collaborations.

## Known Incidents
- APT41 and MSS-linked actors targeting EU pharmaceutical companies during COVID-19 vaccine development (2020)
- Coordinated cyberattack on European Parliament systems (2023), attributed to Chinese actors
- Chinese police operating undeclared overseas "service stations" in multiple EU member states

## Intelligence Implications
China's EU strategy combines economic leverage, technology acquisition, and political influence operations. The main collection priorities are: defence-industrial IP, policy positions on Taiwan and Ukraine, and identification of officials susceptible to cultivation. MSS operations are more patient and less destructive than Russian equivalents.
