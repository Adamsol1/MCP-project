# Russia's Strategic Presence in the Middle East

## Strategic Overview
Russia re-entered the Middle East as a major actor in 2015 with its military intervention in Syria, reversing decades of reduced post-Soviet influence. Russia's Middle East strategy serves multiple objectives: securing warm-water naval access (Tartus), demonstrating military credibility to arms buyers, countering US influence, and building leverage with Gulf energy producers. The Ukraine war has strained Russian capacity but has also deepened partnerships with anti-Western actors.

## Syria: The Foundation of Russian Return
Russia's 2015 military intervention saved the Assad regime from collapse and secured permanent basing rights at Tartus (naval) and Hmeimim (air). Russia deployed advanced air defence systems (S-400, S-300), tested combat systems, and established an operational precedent for expeditionary use of private military contractors (Wagner Group). Tartus provides the Russian Navy's only Mediterranean maintenance facility, critical for projecting naval power into the Atlantic.

## Libya: Wagner Operations
Wagner Group forces deployed to eastern Libya supporting General Haftar's Libyan National Army from 2019 onward. Wagner provided air defence crews, snipers, and logistics, enabling a protracted civil war that gave Russia leverage over European migration routes and Libyan oil. Wagner's Libya presence served as a platform for further expansion into the Sahel (Mali, Central African Republic, Niger).

## Arms Sales as Strategic Tool
Russia is a leading arms supplier to the region, with major customers including Algeria, Egypt, Iraq, and previously Syria. S-400 air defence system sales to Turkey (a NATO member) created significant NATO internal tension. Russian arms relationships create long-term maintenance dependencies and intelligence access through embedded technical personnel. Arms deals provide income insulated from Western sanctions.

## Russia-Iran Alignment
The Ukraine war deepened Russian-Iranian military cooperation. Iran supplied Russia with Shahed-136 kamikaze drones used extensively against Ukrainian infrastructure. In return, Russia has reportedly transferred advanced air defence components, Su-35 aircraft technology, and satellite intelligence to Iran. This represents a qualitative upgrade in Iranian military capability.

## Russia-Gulf State Relations
Despite backing opposing sides in Syria and Yemen, Russia has maintained working relationships with Saudi Arabia and the UAE through OPEC+ coordination. The OPEC+ mechanism gives Russia a seat in global oil price management and creates incentives for Gulf states to resist full alignment with Western pressure campaigns.

## Intelligence Implications
Russian military presence in Syria provides persistent ISR coverage of Eastern Mediterranean, Israeli operations, and US naval movements. Russian-Iranian drone technology transfer represents a direct military risk to US and NATO interests. Wagner's replacement organization (Africa Corps/Russian military) continues operations in Libya with implications for European border security and migrant flows.
