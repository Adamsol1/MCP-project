# North Korea: Strategic Capabilities and Behaviour

## Strategic Overview
The Democratic People's Republic of Korea (DPRK) under Kim Jong-un has pursued a parallel track of nuclear weapons development and economic sanctions evasion with remarkable strategic coherence. North Korea's nuclear programme provides regime survival insurance against US-style regime change operations. Its cyber programme generates hard currency circumventing sanctions. The Russia-Ukraine war has added a new dimension: North Korean arms supply to Russia and reciprocal technology transfer, accelerating DPRK missile and satellite capabilities.

## Nuclear and Missile Programme
North Korea is assessed to possess 40-50 nuclear warheads (as of 2024, estimates vary). It has conducted six nuclear tests and has developed a range of delivery systems:
- **Hwasong-17/18 ICBMs**: Demonstrated range capable of striking any US city; solid-fuel variants reduce launch warning time
- **KN-23/24 short-range ballistic missiles**: Manoeuvrable warhead variants complicate terminal-phase missile defence
- **Submarine-Launched Ballistic Missiles (SLBMs)**: Limited but developing capability from experimental submarines
- **Hypersonic glide vehicles**: In testing, claiming speeds that challenge current missile defence architectures

## Russia Arms Supply (2023–present)
North Korea has supplied Russia with approximately 3 million artillery shells and millions of additional rounds of ammunition for use in Ukraine, according to US, South Korean, and Ukrainian intelligence assessments. North Korean ballistic missiles (KN-23 variants) have been fired against Ukrainian cities. In exchange, Russia has reportedly provided satellite and reconnaissance technology that directly accelerated DPRK's spy satellite programme (Malligyong-1 launched November 2023).

## Cyber Operations — Lazarus Group
North Korea's Reconnaissance General Bureau operates cyber units collectively known as Lazarus Group, APT38, and Kimsuky. The DPRK uses cyber operations primarily for financial theft and weapons technology collection:
- **Cryptocurrency theft**: Responsible for an estimated $1.7 billion in crypto theft in 2022 alone (Chainalysis), funding nuclear programme directly
- **Bank heist methodology**: Bangladesh Bank SWIFT heist (2016, $81M stolen); coordinated global ATM "cash-out" operations
- **Defence sector targeting**: Systematic theft of missile, submarine, and nuclear design information from South Korean and US defence contractors

## Provocations and Escalation Pattern
North Korea uses a cyclical provocation-negotiation-reward pattern: conducting missile tests or nuclear threats to create crisis, then offering partial concessions for sanctions relief or other benefits. Kim Jong-un has accelerated testing pace since 2022, launching more missiles in 2022 than any previous year. North Korea has legally codified its nuclear status and lowered the threshold for first use in its 2022 nuclear law.

## Intelligence Implications
DPRK-Russia military cooperation has qualitatively changed the threat calculus. North Korean ballistic missile technology is being battle-tested in Ukraine, providing empirical performance data. Russian satellite technology transfer accelerates DPRK ISR capability, which in turn improves targeting. The nexus creates a sanctions-evasion loop where North Korea earns geopolitical benefits that money cannot buy.
