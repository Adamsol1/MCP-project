# EU–United States Relations

## Strategic Overview
The EU–US relationship is the world's largest bilateral trade and investment partnership, underpinned by NATO membership for most EU states. Structural tensions exist around technology regulation, trade policy, and burden-sharing — but both sides share threat assessments on Russia and China.

## Key Tension Points

### Technology Regulation Divergence
The EU's GDPR, AI Act, and Digital Markets Act create friction with US technology companies. Transatlantic data transfer frameworks (Privacy Shield → invalidated 2020, EU–US Data Privacy Framework → adopted 2023) remain legally contested. NSA mass surveillance programmes revealed by Snowden continue to influence EU trust calculus.

### Defence Burden-Sharing
US administrations have repeatedly pressed EU NATO members to reach the 2% GDP defence spending target. Only a minority consistently meet it. Post-2022, European defence investment has accelerated but dependency on US capabilities (intelligence, logistics, precision strike) remains high.

### Economic Competition
The US Inflation Reduction Act (2022) introduced subsidies that EU manufacturers view as discriminatory. Semiconductor supply chain competition (CHIPS Acts on both sides) is driving parallel industrial strategies that may conflict. Tariff disputes over steel, aluminium, and aircraft remain unresolved.

### Intelligence Cooperation
The Five Eyes framework (US, UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand) does not include EU member states as members, creating asymmetric intelligence sharing. Bilateral arrangements with key EU states (Germany, France, Netherlands) partially compensate but create uneven access.

## Known Incidents
- EU parliament data exposed via US-linked collection programmes (Snowden disclosures, 2013)
- US intelligence collection against German Chancellor's communications (confirmed 2015)
- Ongoing disputes over Palantir and US technology firms' access to EU government data

## Intelligence Implications
Despite alliance tensions, EU–US intelligence coordination on Russian and Chinese threats is operationally close. The primary risks are political: US election outcomes that shift commitment to NATO, and technology policy disputes that create legal barriers to operational data sharing.
