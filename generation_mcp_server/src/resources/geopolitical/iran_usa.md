# Iran–United States Relations

## Strategic Overview
The US-Iran relationship has been defined by antagonism since the 1979 Islamic Revolution and the hostage crisis. The relationship oscillated between limited diplomatic engagement (JCPOA 2015) and maximum pressure (2018–present), with Iran's nuclear programme, proxy networks, and regional behaviour remaining the core points of conflict. Both states view each other in existential terms for their respective regional orders.

## Nuclear Programme
Iran's nuclear programme is the defining strategic concern. The 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) capped enrichment at 3.67% and reduced stockpiles in exchange for sanctions relief. The US unilaterally withdrew in May 2018 under the Trump administration, citing the deal's sunset clauses and Iran's missile programme. Iran subsequently broke all JCPOA limits, enriching uranium to 60% (just below weapons-grade of 90%) and accumulating stockpiles sufficient for multiple weapons if further enriched. As of 2024, Iran's nuclear "breakout time" — the time required to produce enough weapons-grade material for one bomb — is assessed at under two weeks by US and Israeli intelligence.

## Proxy Network
Iran projects power through a "Axis of Resistance" — a network of armed non-state actors across the region:
- **Hezbollah** (Lebanon): Iran's most capable proxy, with an estimated 150,000 rockets and precision-guided missiles
- **Houthis** (Yemen): Conducts drone and missile attacks on shipping in the Red Sea and Gulf of Aden, threatening global trade routes
- **Kata'ib Hezbollah and PMF** (Iraq/Syria): Targets US forces; conducted attacks killing US personnel in Jordan (2024)
- **Hamas and Islamic Jihad** (Gaza): Receives Iranian weapons, financing, and training

## Sanctions Regime
The US maintains the most comprehensive sanctions regime against Iran of any country outside North Korea. Sanctions target oil exports, banking, shipping, and arms transfers. Iran circumvents sanctions through oil sales to China (significant volumes), use of front companies, cryptocurrency, and shadow shipping fleets.

## Cyber Conflict
Iran has conducted significant cyber operations against US and allied targets since at least 2010, including the Shamoon attacks against Saudi Aramco (2012), attacks on US financial sector (Operation Ababil), and intrusions into critical infrastructure. Iranian cyber actors have targeted US election infrastructure, government networks, and defence contractors.

## Intelligence Implications
US-Iran tensions create persistent risk of miscalculation. Iranian proxy attacks on US forces, Israeli operations against Iranian nuclear scientists and facilities, and US/Israeli sabotage operations (Stuxnet, Natanz explosion 2020) maintain a shadow war temperature that could escalate rapidly. Any escalation in the nuclear file or proxy attacks on US personnel risks conventional military exchange.
