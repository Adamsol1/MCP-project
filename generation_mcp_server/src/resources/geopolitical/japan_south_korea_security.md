# Japan and South Korea: Security Posture and Alliance Dynamics

## Strategic Overview
Japan and South Korea are the US's two primary treaty allies in Northeast Asia, providing basing and forward presence essential to US deterrence in the Indo-Pacific. Both face direct North Korean missile and nuclear threats. Japan faces additional pressure from China over the Senkaku/Diaoyu Islands and Taiwan contingency planning. Historical grievances between Tokyo and Seoul — rooted in Japan's colonial occupation — have periodically impaired alliance coordination, though trilateral security cooperation has strengthened significantly since 2022.

## Japan's Rearmament
Japan's security posture has undergone its most significant revision since the post-war Constitution. The Kishida (2021-2024) and Ishiba administrations have:
- Committed to doubling defence spending to 2% of GDP by 2027 (from ~1%)
- Developed "counterstrike capability" — land-attack cruise missiles (Tomahawk and domestically produced variants) enabling pre-emptive strikes on missile launch sites
- Expanded the Japan Maritime Self-Defence Force with new destroyers and a quasi-carrier programme (Izumo-class helicopter carriers modified to carry F-35B)
- Revised national security strategy documents (2022) naming China as an "unprecedented strategic challenge" and Russia a "grave concern"

## Japan-China: Senkaku Islands
Japan administers the uninhabited Senkaku Islands (called Diaoyu by China), which sit above potentially significant hydrocarbon deposits. Chinese Coast Guard vessels regularly enter the territorial waters around the Senkakus, with Japan dispatching Coast Guard and Self-Defence Force vessels in response. China's 2021 Coast Guard Law authorised its vessels to use weapons against foreign ships in claimed waters — directly escalating the Senkaku risk.

## South Korea's Evolving Posture
South Korea (ROK) has modernised its military significantly, deploying THAAD (Terminal High Altitude Area Defense) missile batteries in 2017, which triggered a prolonged Chinese economic retaliation campaign (tourism bans, trade restrictions). ROK maintains a policy of extended deterrence reliance on the US nuclear umbrella and has domestic debate about developing independent nuclear weapons capability. ROK-US joint exercises have resumed at full scale after the Trump-era suspension.

## QUAD and Regional Frameworks
Japan is a core QUAD member (with the US, Australia, India), which coordinates on technology, supply chains, infrastructure, and maritime security. AUKUS (Australia-UK-US) involves Japan in some technology dimensions. Japan has signed defence partnerships with Australia, the UK, and France. South Korea has limited QUAD involvement due to concerns about Chinese retaliation and a more cautious China policy.

## Trilateral US-Japan-ROK Cooperation
The Camp David trilateral summit (August 2023) established annual leader summits, real-time military data sharing for North Korean missile threats, and joint exercises. This institutionalised cooperation represents the strongest trilateral alignment in decades and was directly driven by North Korea's accelerated missile programme and the Russia-Ukraine war's regional lessons.

## Intelligence Implications
Japan and South Korea represent the forward line of any Taiwan or Korean Peninsula contingency. Both have robust intelligence services with significant SIGINT capability targeting China and North Korea. Japan's new counterstrike capability changes the regional deterrence equation — China must now factor Japanese strike capability into Taiwan contingency planning, not just US forces.
