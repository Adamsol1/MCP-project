# Gulf States–China Relations

## Strategic Overview
China has emerged as the largest trade partner for the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) states, displacing the United States in several bilateral relationships. Gulf-China ties are built primarily on energy — China is the world's largest oil importer and Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Iraq are among its top suppliers. The relationship has deepened into technology, military sales, and financial arrangements that create friction with US regional strategy.

## Energy-for-Technology Exchange

### Saudi Arabia
Saudi Arabia supplies approximately 15-17% of China's crude oil imports. Beyond oil, the relationship has expanded to include Chinese participation in Vision 2030 industrial projects, Huawei 5G infrastructure deployment, and discussions about settling oil trades in Chinese yuan (RMB/petrodollar challenge). SABIC-Sinopec joint ventures represent deep industrial integration.

### UAE
The UAE has been the most aggressive Gulf state in adopting Chinese technology. Huawei infrastructure is deeply embedded in UAE telecoms; the US has repeatedly pressured Abu Dhabi to remove Huawei from networks used by US military forces at Al Dhafra Air Base. In 2021, the US threatened to withhold F-35 sales unless the UAE excluded Huawei — a deal that ultimately collapsed. CNOOC and Adnoc have signed major energy partnership agreements.

## Military Dimension
China has sold armed drones (Wing Loong series) to the UAE and Saudi Arabia, circumventing US export restrictions that limited American drone transfers. Saudi Arabia has constructed ballistic missile facilities with apparent Chinese technical assistance, according to US intelligence assessments. China's first overseas naval logistics facility is in Djibouti, adjacent to US and French bases.

## Yuan Oil Settlement Pressure
Saudi Arabia and other Gulf states have conducted limited oil trades in yuan, and the Shanghai Petroleum and Natural Gas Exchange has processed some Gulf transactions in RMB. While the dollar-denominated petrodollar system remains dominant, Chinese pressure to diversify oil settlement currency is a long-term structural challenge to US financial leverage.

## US-China Competition in the Gulf
The Gulf states are deliberately hedging between the US (security guarantor, primary arms supplier) and China (largest economic partner, technology provider). Saudi Arabia and the UAE have pursued Chinese engagement on issues where US support was withheld, including during the Yemen war and the COVID-19 vaccine period. The Abraham Accords reflected US-Israeli-Gulf alignment, but China brokered a Saudi-Iran diplomatic normalisation in 2023 — a significant display of Chinese diplomatic reach in a US-dominated region.

## Intelligence Implications
Chinese telecommunications infrastructure embedded in Gulf networks creates persistent collection risk for any US, European, or Israeli operations conducted through those environments. Gulf-China military and financial integration reduces US leverage in crises and creates information security concerns for multinational companies and governments operating regionally.
