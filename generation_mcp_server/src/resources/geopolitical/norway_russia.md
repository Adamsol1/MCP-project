# Norway–Russia Relations

## Strategic Overview
Norway shares a 198 km land border with Russia in the High North, making it the only NATO member with a direct border with Russia in the Arctic. This geographic reality defines much of Norway's security posture and threat calculus.

## Key Tension Points

### Svalbard
The Svalbard archipelago is governed by Norway under the 1920 Svalbard Treaty, but Russia maintains a settlement at Barentsburg. Russia has consistently challenged Norwegian administrative authority on the islands, particularly around fishing rights in the surrounding Fisheries Protection Zone. Russian rhetoric frames Norwegian governance as a violation of treaty rights.

### Barents Sea and Arctic Resources
The 2010 Barents Sea delimitation treaty resolved a decades-long border dispute, but competition over Arctic energy resources and shipping lanes remains active. As ice retreats, the Northern Sea Route becomes increasingly strategic — Russia seeks to control it as a national waterway, while Norway (and NATO) assert freedom of navigation.

### Military Activity
Russia's Northern Fleet, headquartered at Severomorsk on the Kola Peninsula, is less than 50 km from the Norwegian border. Norway monitors significant Russian military exercises in the region, including submarine patrols, air incursions near Norwegian airspace, and signals intelligence activities targeting Norwegian infrastructure.

## Known Incidents
- Repeated GPS jamming affecting northern Norway, particularly during Russian military exercises (2017–present)
- Russian fishing vessels used for intelligence collection near Norwegian offshore installations
- Cyberattacks attributed to Russian actors against the Storting (Norwegian parliament) in 2020 and 2021

## Infrastructure Vulnerabilities
Norwegian oil and gas infrastructure in the Barents Sea and North Sea is considered a high-value target. The Hammerfest LNG facility and subsea pipelines to continental Europe are of particular concern. The sabotage of Nord Stream 1 and 2 in September 2022 demonstrated the viability of such attacks.

## Intelligence Implications
Norwegian intelligence (PST, E-tjenesten) assesses Russia as the primary state-level threat. Russia has both motive (energy leverage, territorial disputes) and capability (GRU, SVR, FSB) to conduct hybrid operations targeting Norwegian critical infrastructure, political institutions, and military readiness.
