# Norway–China Relations

## Strategic Overview
Norway–China relations were frozen from 2010 to 2016 following the Nobel Peace Prize awarded to dissident Liu Xiaobo. The subsequent normalisation introduced new economic interdependencies alongside persistent security concerns, particularly around Chinese technology penetration and investment in strategic sectors.

## Key Tension Points

### Huawei and Telecom Infrastructure
Norway excluded Huawei from its 5G rollout in 2023 following pressure from allies and assessments by the Norwegian National Security Authority (NSM). Telenor had previously relied on Huawei equipment in its 4G network. The transition represents both a security gain and a diplomatic friction point with Beijing.

### Seafood and Economic Leverage
China was Norway's largest seafood export market before 2010. Economic coercion following the Nobel Prize demonstrated China's willingness to use trade as a political instrument. The 2016 normalisation restored trade but established a precedent for economic pressure.

### Arctic and Shipping
China has declared itself a "near-Arctic state" and seeks observer status and influence in Arctic governance. Chinese research vessels operate in Norwegian maritime zones. Norway monitors Chinese interest in Arctic shipping routes and scientific installations that may have dual-use intelligence value.

### Investment Screening
Chinese investment in Norwegian maritime, energy, and technology sectors has increased scrutiny under Norway's National Security Act (2019). Several acquisitions have been reviewed or blocked on national security grounds.

## Known Incidents
- Chinese state-linked actors suspected in reconnaissance of Norwegian maritime and energy companies (NSM annual threat assessments 2021–2023)
- Phishing campaigns targeting Norwegian research institutions with Arctic or defence ties, attributed to Chinese APT groups
- Attempted acquisition of strategic port facilities reviewed under security legislation

## Intelligence Implications
China's primary interest in Norway is technological and economic — acquiring maritime technology, fisheries expertise, and Arctic knowledge. Secondary interests include monitoring NATO member positions and exploiting Norway's role as an energy supplier to Europe. MSS and APT41-linked actors are assessed as the primary cyber threat vectors.
