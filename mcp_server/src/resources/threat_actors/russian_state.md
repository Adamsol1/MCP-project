# Russian State-Sponsored Threat Actors

## Strategic Overview
Russia operates the most aggressive and diverse state cyber capability of any nation. Three primary intelligence services run distinct cyber programmes: the GRU (military intelligence), FSB (domestic/counterintelligence), and SVR (foreign intelligence). These services compete as well as cooperate, resulting in overlapping operations against the same targets.

## Primary Threat Groups

### GRU — APT28 / Fancy Bear
Military intelligence unit 26165. Primary focus: political and military intelligence collection, election interference, and influence operations. Known operations include the DNC breach (2016), targeting of the WADA anti-doping agency, and systematic attacks against EU political parties and governments. TTPs: spearphishing, credential harvesting, X-Agent malware.

### GRU — Sandworm / Unit 74455
The GRU's most destructive unit. Responsible for the most damaging cyberattacks in history: NotPetya (2017, $10 billion in damages globally), Ukrainian power grid attacks (2015–2016), and Olympic Destroyer (2018 Winter Olympics). Demonstrated ICS/SCADA attack capability. TTPs: destructive wiper malware, ICS exploitation, supply chain compromise.

### FSB — Turla / Snake / Venomous Bear
Long-running collection operation active since at least 2004. Focus on government, military, and diplomatic targets across Europe, Middle East, and Central Asia. Known for extraordinary persistence — Snake implants have survived years on target systems. TTPs: rootkits, satellite communication hijacking for C2, watering-hole attacks.

### SVR — APT29 / Cozy Bear
Russia's foreign intelligence service cyber arm. Focus on long-term, stealthy collection against government, think-tank, and technology targets. Responsible for SolarWinds supply chain compromise (2020), Microsoft executive email access (2024), and sustained operations against Western intelligence services. TTPs: living off the land, legitimate tool abuse, supply chain compromise.

## Known Major Operations
- SolarWinds compromise affecting 18,000 organisations including US Treasury and State Department (SVR, 2019–2020)
- NotPetya destructive attack causing $10B+ global damages, delivered via Ukrainian accounting software (GRU, 2017)
- Interference in US (2016), French (2017), and German (2017) elections (GRU/IRA)

## Intelligence Implications
Russian cyber operations are integrated into broader hybrid warfare doctrine combining cyber, influence operations, and physical sabotage. Operations are often nested — cyber collection enabling physical sabotage targeting, or influence operations amplifying the effect of breaches. Attribution is complicated by deliberate false flag operations.
